# Hook Skills

## 0. Core definition

A good Hook is not a “catchy sentence”.  
A good Hook creates psychological tension in 1–3 seconds, makes the viewer feel “this is about me”, and gives them a low-friction reason to continue.

The strongest Hook usually does four things:

1. It triggers a desire, fear, pain, comparison, self-interest, or curiosity.
2. It makes the next action simple: keep watching, click, read, comment, save, or compare.
3. It promises an uncertain but attractive reward: answer, reveal, method, result, contrast, mistake, checklist, proof.
4. It creates a small investment: attention, comment, save, identity, learning progress, purchase intent, or product familiarity.

## 1. Source model

Use the Hook addiction loop from the uploaded video:

Trigger → Action → Reward → Investment

The video’s deeper extraction:

- Trigger’s hook is Desire.
- Action’s hook is Simplicity.
- Reward’s hook is Luck / Uncertainty.
- Investment’s hook is Wealth / Accumulated value.

For content and ad copy, translate this as:

- Desire: remind users what they want, fear, lack, compare, or secretly care about.
- Simplicity: make continuation easy; avoid cognitive load.
- Uncertainty: leave a useful gap; do not reveal everything too soon.
- Wealth: make the user feel their attention, learning, comment, save, or product evaluation is accumulating value.

## 2. Hook operating formula

Default formula:

Target user + specific scene + hidden pain point + human trigger + curiosity gap + product-relevant direction

Example:

“如果你每天涂精华，但皮肤还是没变化，问题可能不是精华不够贵。”

Breakdown:

- Target user: daily skincare users.
- Scene: applying serum every day.
- Hidden pain: effort without visible change.
- Trigger: loss aversion, frustration, self-doubt.
- Curiosity gap: what is the real reason?
- Product direction: absorption, skincare routine, microneedling education.

## 3. Must-pass quality criteria

A Hook is not qualified unless it passes these checks:

1. Specific person: the viewer can quickly identify themselves.
2. Specific scene: not abstract, not slogan-like.
3. One main emotional trigger: do not mix too many emotions in one line.
4. Clear tension: pain, contradiction, comparison, risk, desire, or missing answer.
5. Product relevance: the next content can naturally connect to the product.
6. Truthfulness: no exaggerated medical, financial, or absolute claims.
7. Low friction: the viewer understands the sentence immediately.
8. Expandability: the line can naturally lead into 10–60 seconds of content.

## 4. Output format when generating hooks

When asked to generate Hooks, output a table or structured list with:

- Hook line
- Human trigger
- Pain point / curiosity gap
- Product connection
- Best use case: ad / UGC / SEO intro / landing page / email / product card
- Claim risk: Low / Medium / High
- Suggested follow-up content

## 5. Output format when auditing hooks

Score each Hook from 1–5 on:

- Specificity
- Emotional force
- Curiosity gap
- Product relevance
- Credibility
- Compliance safety
- First-3-second clarity

Then provide:

- Main problem
- Better version
- Why the better version works
- Risk warning if any

## 6. Human trigger bank

Use these triggers responsibly:

- Fear: risk, mistake, loss, worsening, regret.
- Greed / gain: deal, advantage, shortcut, extra value.
- Laziness: simpler, less effort, fewer steps, done-for-you.
- Envy / comparison: others already know, peer contrast, same age different result.
- Aspiration: become a better version, expert routine, lifestyle upgrade.
- Attraction / beauty: confidence, close distance, appearance, first impression.
- Self-interest: money, time, face, safety, control, certainty.
- Kindness / care: family, partner, pet, gifting, protection, support.

## 7. Ethical boundary

Do not create hooks that rely on deception, fabricated proof, fake scarcity, shame attacks, illegal claims, medical promises, or manipulative targeting of vulnerable users.

For beauty/skincare/microneedling, avoid:

- “cure”, “treat disease”, “guaranteed”, “permanent”, “doctor-level result”
- implying medical efficacy without evidence
- promising scar removal, acne cure, wrinkle elimination, hair regrowth, or collagen increase as guaranteed outcomes

Prefer safer language:

- “may help support”
- “designed for”
- “routine”
- “visible-looking”
- “appearance of”
- “learn before you try”
- “not everyone is suitable”
- “follow the instructions”
- “patch test / hygiene / aftercare”
